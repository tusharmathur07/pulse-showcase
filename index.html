I · The Statement

Every owner makes the biggest decisions of their life flying blind

Every small business owner and startup founder makes the most important financial decisions of their life — hire, spend, expand, survive — without any of the data or guidance they need to make them well.

This is not a technology problem. There is no shortage of accounting software — QuickBooks has 4.5 million customers, Xero has millions more, and the plumbing (Stripe, ADP, Plaid) exists. The problem is that all of these tools are built to record what happened, not to tell you what is about to happen and what to do about it. A full-time CFO would do that work, and a full-time CFO costs $200,000–$280,000 per year. The 33 million small businesses in North America under $5M in revenue cannot afford one.

Pulse exists to close that gap. Not as accounting software. Not as another dashboard. As the system that watches the financial pulse of a business continuously, detects what is wrong before it becomes a crisis, and tells the owner — in plain language, on their phone, without requiring them to log in to anything — what is happening and what options they have.

II · The Problem, in Full

Six documented failure modes

1

The cash gap arrives without warning

A restaurant owner checks her account on Friday and realises payroll runs in four days and she can't cover it. QuickBooks showed her last month. It showed her nothing about next week. Cash flow failure is cited as the primary cause of 82% of small business failures — most preventable with 2–3 weeks of earlier visibility.

2

The owner has no one to ask

A contractor deciding whether to take an $800,000 project needing $200,000 upfront has one question — can I float this? — and no one to call. His accountant files taxes annually; a fractional CFO costs $3,000/month minimum. So he decides by gut. The fractional CFO market is growing 17% annually in Canada: that growth is unmet demand.

3

The tax remittance arrives as a surprise

Every quarter, tens of thousands of Canadian owners get their GST/HST notice and discover they owe money they never set aside. No tool tells them, weekly, how much tax they're accumulating and how much to reserve. It's not carelessness — it's a tools failure.

4

Invoices sit unpaid and nobody chases them

An agency owner has $87,000 outstanding but doesn't know which clients are genuinely stuck versus chronically late. A 25-day delay on $87,000 at 12% cost of capital costs ~$720/month — a number that appears nowhere in QuickBooks. The cost of slow collections is invisible, so it never gets addressed.

5

The founder can't answer basic investor questions

A pre-seed founder tells an angel "about 9 months" of runway — a number from a three-week-old spreadsheet. The real answer today is 6.5 months, because of a hiring decision she hasn't modelled. The startup language differs (burn, runway, MRR) but the danger is identical to the restaurant owner's.

6

Existing tools require the owner to come to them

QuickBooks, Float, Relay, Xero — all require the owner to open the app and interpret what they see. A restaurant owner working 70-hour weeks does not check dashboards. The moment that always happens is Tuesday morning, phone in hand, 45 minutes before opening. The product must come to them at that moment.

III · The Gap We Target

The space between intelligence and action

The gap is not in accounting software (saturated) or bookkeeping (dozens of good tools). It is between intelligence and action — and it has three specific dimensions.

Dimension 1

No proactive delivery

Every tool waits to be opened. Pulse comes to the owner — SMS or email, no login.

Dimension 2

No execution layer

Tools tell owners what is wrong. Pulse lets them fix it in one tap — a reminder sent to the slow client from inside the alert.

Dimension 3

No Canadian tax intelligence

No tool in this category reserves GST/HST proactively. Pulse does.

IV · The Solution, Step by Step

From signup to the SMS that changes how they run the business

Nineteen steps across five stages — every step described as it actually functions. After setup, the owner does almost nothing.

Step 1 · Connect in 10 minutes

QuickBooks/Xero via OAuth (read-only), bank via Plaid (tokenised, no credentials stored), payroll (ADP Run / QB Payroll / Gusto), optional revenue (Square/Toast/Stripe/Shopify), and an 8-question setup the data can't reveal — cash threshold, tax rate, business type, payroll frequency, contact preference. Four minutes.

Step 2 · Build the model (2am nightly)

Deterministic Python — not AI. A 24-month seasonal baseline, a per-client payment-delay model (Client A always pays day 16, not day 0), a 90-day cash flow loop of exact inflows and outflows, and a separate GST/HST reserve calculation.

Step 3 · Intelligence detection

Cash gap detection scans the forecast; z-score anomaly detection flags what's unusual for this business (food cost 31%→39%, not a generic benchmark); slow-receivables detection compares each client to their own pattern; a priority engine surfaces at most two signals a week.

Step 4 · Language generation

Claude enters only here. The system assembles a JSON object of pre-computed numbers; Claude writes ≤90 words from those numbers, addressing the owner by name with numbered options. It cannot invent a figure it wasn't given.

Step 5 · Delivery & execution

Validated alerts go out by SMS (Twilio) or email (SendGrid) at 8am Tuesday and Thursday — no login. A one-tap link sends an invoice reminder directly to a slow client from the owner's email. The alert and the action become one moment.

Output validation

Before any alert sends, a function scans every dollar figure in Claude's output and confirms it matches a figure in the input JSON — even a rounding difference triggers a rejection and regeneration. This makes a hallucinated number architecturally impossible to deliver.

The Q&A layer lets the owner ask at any time — "Can I afford to hire a manager at $55,000?" "What if my biggest client pays 30 days late?" — and get an answer computed by the scenario engine, written in plain language.

VII · The Foundation

Architectural principles

The LLM is a translator, not a calculator

Every dollar figure is produced by deterministic Python before the LLM sees it. Output validation confirms every number before delivery.

Push-first, pull-optional

The core value is delivered through proactive alerts that arrive without login. A customer who reads every SMS and never opens the app is more valuable than one who logs in once and churns.

Intelligence, then execution

An alert that tells you what to do is good; one that lets you do it in one tap is what makes the product sticky.

Canadian-first, not as an afterthought

ADP Run for payroll (not Gusto). GST/HST reserves from day one. Data in AWS Canada (Montreal). PIPEDA from day one.

Vertical depth over horizontal breadth

A restaurant product that knows food cost %, covers per hour, and labour benchmarks is 10x more valuable than a generic tool. That depth is the moat.

Accuracy is the product, not a feature

One wrong forecast that drives a real bad decision, in a tight community like Vancouver's restaurant scene, can end the product. Confidence intervals and validation are existential.

The one architectural rule that matters above all others

The LLM never touches a number. The math never touches language.

Deterministic Python math handles all financial calculations. Claude handles only natural language. A number that appears in any alert was computed by the Python model before the LLM ever saw it — it receives a structured JSON object and writes sentences from those numbers. It cannot invent figures it was not given. This is the core safety guarantee of the entire product.

Part 0 · The Z-Score Foundation

One statistic that self-normalises across every business

Seven of the fifteen metrics use z-scores. A fixed threshold doesn't work because every business has a different normal — a restaurant that always runs 38% food cost is not in trouble at 38%; one that normally runs 30% and hits 38% this week is. The z-score measures distance from the business's own historical mean in units of its own standard deviation.

1 — Historical observations: x₁, x₂, ..., xₙ (Pulse uses n = 12 weeks) 2 — Arithmetic mean: mean = sum(xᵢ) / n 3 — Sample standard deviation: variance = sum((xᵢ − mean)²) / (n − 1) stdev = sqrt(variance) (n − 1 = Bessel's correction) 4 — Z-score: z = (x_current − mean) / stdev 5 — Alert threshold: |z| > 2.0 → fire

Why Bessel's correction (n−1)?

Dividing by n underestimates the true population variance from a sample, because the mean is itself estimated from the same data. With n = 12, dividing by 11 gives an unbiased estimator. Source: William Gosset (Student), 1908.

Why z = 2.0, and the 4.6% false-positive rate

Under a normal distribution, ~95.4% of observations fall within ±2σ (the empirical rule), leaving ~4.6% beyond — the expected false-positive rate per metric per week. A threshold of 1.5 would raise false positives to 13.4%; 2.5 would cut them to 1.2% but miss early signals. Source: de Moivre (1733), Laplace, Gauss.

M-01 · Projected Cash Gap — HIGH›

Discipline: Cash flow forecasting / discrete-time financial modelling. Source: corporate treasury management; the discrete daily loop parallels Basel III LCR's 30-day forward projection.

balance[0] = current_bank_balance (Plaid balance.current — the only observed number) balance[day] = balance[day−1] + inflows[day] − outflows[day] for day = 1..90 inflows = confirmed_deposits + expected_invoice_receipts + projected_revenue outflows = payroll_debit + scheduled_bills + recurring_fixed_costs payroll_debit = net_pay + employer_taxes; employer_taxes = gross × (0.0595 + 0.0166) cash_threshold = max(3000, 0.50 × avg_weekly_fixed_costs) gap_date = min(day where balance[day] < cash_threshold)

A first-order linear recurrence: each day's balance depends only on the prior day plus net cash flows. The only observed number is the starting balance — every other number is derived. Confidence HIGH: the formula is exact arithmetic; uncertainty lives in the inputs, handled with explicit confidence intervals.

Post-revision statusModel · exact engine, estimated inputs

Arithmetic is exact and trustworthy; the output is now honest about its own uncertainty. Highly accurate inside 7–14 days, but capped by the seasonal model (M-10) beyond 30 days. Confident in the mechanism, cautiously confident near-term, explicitly uncertain long-horizon — the correct posture.

Discrepancies found: the loop produced a single point estimate dressed as certain while every input is itself an estimate; irregular real outflows (annual insurance, quarterly tax, one-off equipment) were silently missing; the "confidence intervals" the text referenced were never actually built.

How we fixed it
  • Kept the exact daily recurrence but run it 1,000 times with inputs varied (revenue ±15%, payment timing from the client delay model, cost noise ±10%).
  • Output changed from one number to a probability and a band: "X% chance of breaching the floor between day A and B," with a 10th–90th percentile range on the gap.
  • Added an unmodelled-outflow scan over 12+ months of history that lists what the forecast is NOT capturing instead of dropping it.
Still needs attention
  • Band width is only as good as the assumed input distributions; the ±15% and delay model need calibration against real businesses.
  • The unmodelled-outflow scan needs a real "irregular and large" detection rule tuned on actual histories.
  • Cannot be more reliable than M-10, so it is gated on M-10 improving.
M-02 · Payroll Coverage Ratio — VERY HIGH›

Discipline: Financial ratio analysis / liquidity coverage ratios. Source: debt service coverage ratio; Basel III LCR adapted to payroll.

payroll_cash_out = net_pay + employer_taxes employer_taxes = gross × 0.0595 (CPP) + gross × 0.0166 (EI) = gross × 0.0761 payroll_coverage = balance[payroll_check_date] / payroll_cash_out warn < 1.20 critical < 1.00

Both net pay and employer taxes leave the account on the same day, so the obligation is net + employer taxes, not gross. The 1.20 warning buffer is calibrated to forecast uncertainty; the 1.00 critical line is exact arithmetic. Source: CRA 2026 CPP/EI contribution rates.

Post-revision statusModel · inherits M-01

Among the most reliable formulas in the system (confirmed payroll data, simple division). Accuracy tracks M-01's forecast in the numerator: very accurate within a week, as uncertain as the forecast further out. The lower-bound comparison makes it appropriately conservative — the right direction for a survival metric.

Discrepancies found: the numerator is the M-01 forecast, so it carried all of M-01's uncertainty while being compared against fixed threshold lines as if certain; variable pay (tips, commissions) was estimated with a flat 8-week average, understating the obligation in a quiet month before a busy payroll.

How we fixed it
  • We now compare the LOWER bound of the projected balance (10th percentile from M-01's simulation) against 1.0, so a genuinely at-risk payroll stops hiding behind a comfortable point estimate.
  • Variable pay is now estimated with a seasonally-weighted figure instead of a flat 8-week mean.
  • The number carries a confidence tier based on days-to-run: tight inside 7 days, widened beyond 30.
Still needs attention
  • The seasonal weighting for tips/commissions needs real variable-pay data to calibrate.
  • Like its dependence on M-01, long-horizon reliability is gated on the forecast improving.
M-03 · GST/HST Tax Reserve Gap — MEDIUM-HIGH›

Discipline: Tax liability estimation / accrual accounting. Source: CRA Excise Tax Act; Input Tax Credit framework (IT-518); CRA RC4022.

est_gst_owing = taxable_revenue_this_quarter × gst_rate BC = 0.05 ON = 0.13 AB = 0.05 QC = 0.14975 est_ITCs = sum(business_expense × gst_rate) on taxable purchases est_gst_owing_net = est_gst_owing − est_ITCs weekly_reserve_needed = (est_gst_owing_net − gst_reserved) / weeks_remaining alert when weeks_remaining < 8 AND gap > $500

ITCs are GST paid on business inputs, claimable against the remittance. Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH: gross owing is exact given accurate revenue; the ITC estimate carries uncertainty, treated conservatively (slightly overstated) — the right direction for a reserve.

Post-revision statusSimple arithmetic · categorisation risk

The core math is simple and now points in the safe direction. Before the fix it under-reserved — the exact harm the feature exists to prevent; after, it errs toward over-reserving, which is correct for a tax-reserve tool. Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH, with the residual error in the safe direction.

Discrepancies found: ITCs were estimated by applying the full GST rate to every categorised expense, which over-counts — wages, most insurance, interest and exempt/zero-rated purchases carry no recoverable GST, and meals are only 50% recoverable; a single flat provincial rate was applied even for businesses selling across provinces.

How we fixed it
  • Replaced the blanket rate with a per-category eligibility factor: full credit on standard taxable inputs, half on meals/entertainment, zero on wages/insurance/interest/exempt items.
  • For multi-province sellers the rate is now keyed to place of supply, not the home province.
  • LLM-categorised (lower-confidence) expenses default to zero eligibility until confirmed — biasing the reserve toward reserving more.
Still needs attention
  • Eligibility factors are a reasonable default but the real recoverable fraction varies by business; validate against actual filed returns where available.
  • The 30% LLM-fallback share directly affects the ITC estimate, so it is coupled to categorisation accuracy improving.
M-04 · Gross Margin % — HIGH›

Discipline: Managerial accounting / contribution analysis. Source: Horngren, Datar & Rajan; Graham, Dodd & Cottle. Z-layer from Shewhart SPC (Bell Labs, 1924).

gross_margin_pct = (Revenue − COGS) / Revenue × 100 z_margin = (gm_this_week − rolling_12wk_mean) / rolling_12wk_stdev alert when z_margin < −2.0 (negative direction only)

The percentage form normalises for business size. Only a fall below the lower control limit alerts — rising margin is not a problem. Confidence HIGH: exact arithmetic on P&L data; gross margin over 12 weeks approximates normality well enough for the 2σ threshold.

Post-revision statusHigh for the level · good for the alert

The margin percentage itself is exact and reliable. The alert layer is now substantially better behaved — the sigma floor removes the most common false-alarm cause. Confident in the reported margin, reasonably confident in the alert pending real-data confirmation of the false-positive rate.

Discrepancies found: margin is bounded and, for a stable business, sits in a very tight band, so a tiny standard deviation made the alert band absurdly narrow and tripped on ordinary moves; COGS posts on a lag and in lumps, so weekly margin swung on bookkeeping timing rather than real performance.

How we fixed it
  • Floored the standard deviation at a metric-specific minimum (about 1 percentage point) so a steady business is not punished for being steady.
  • When COGS posting is stale or lumpy this week, we smooth to a trailing 4-week margin and suppress the raw-week z-alert.
Still needs attention
  • The sigma-floor value and the COGS-staleness trigger need tuning on real P&L feeds.
  • Like all z-metrics, its true false-positive rate can only be measured against real businesses in shadow mode.
M-05 · Food Cost % — HIGH / MED›

Discipline: Restaurant managerial accounting. Source: Miller, Hayes & Dopson; NRA Annual Report; BCRFA benchmarks.

food_cost_pct = food_COGS / food_revenue × 100 food_cost_pct + food_gross_margin_pct = 100% z_food = (fc_this_week − rolling_12wk_mean) / rolling_12wk_stdev alert when z_food > +2.0 OR food_cost_pct > 40% (absolute danger zone)

POS data preferred over accounting for a 48-hour timing advantage. Positive-direction alert (high cost is bad). Confidence HIGH for z-score, MEDIUM for the 40% ceiling — fine-dining concepts may legitimately run higher, so the z-score is the more reliable per-business signal.

Post-revision statusHigh for the level & the moderate alert

The cleanest metric in the system. The level is exact, and the alert is now honest about the difference between a believable cost rise and a likely data glitch. Confident here — the remaining uncertainty is operational (period-matching of deliveries), not mathematical.

Discrepancies found: the framework treated a very large z-score as very high confidence, but a z that large is near-impossible under the model's own assumption — it indicates a data error (double-counted invoice, POS remap, bulk stock-up), not an overnight cost blowout; food received one week and sold the next mismatched the cost and revenue periods.

How we fixed it
  • Cap the z used for confidence and re-route anything beyond about 4σ to a "verify this data" path rather than asserting a cost crisis.
  • Believable cost rises (z 2–4) still alert normally; the "spike with no matching supplier invoice = waste/theft" flag is retained, and a spike WITH an outsized invoice is now treated as a possible bulk-buy timing issue.
Still needs attention
  • The 4σ "verify data" cutoff is a sensible default but should be confirmed against real restaurant data.
  • Period-matching of deliveries to sales weeks is an ingestion concern at the data layer, not a formula concern.
M-06 · Labour Cost % — HIGH›

Discipline: Operational efficiency ratios / accrual-basis management accounting. Source: Miller, Hayes & Dopson.

labor_pct = total_payroll_expense (accrual) / total_revenue × 100 accrual = hours_worked × rate (NOT the cash payroll debit) z_labor = (lp_this_week − rolling_12wk_mean) / rolling_12wk_stdev Compound signal (escalated): z_labor > +1.5 AND revenue_z < −1.0

Accrual basis is essential — biweekly cash payroll produces two pays in some months and zero in others; accrual labour is constant week to week. The compound signal catches the worst case (costs rising while revenue falls) even when neither crosses 2.0 alone. Confidence HIGH.

Post-revision statusGood · improved

When hours data is present, the metric is accurate. The fallback is now free of the calendar saw-tooth, so the no-hours case is materially better. The compound signal's two-week persistence rule collapses its false-positive rate. Reasonably confident, pending real-data tuning.

Discrepancies found: when hours data is missing, the fallback divided monthly payroll by weeks-in-month — reintroducing the exact biweekly distortion it set out to remove (a three-payday month looked ~50% heavier); the compound signal multiplied two noisy 12-week estimates and could fire on a one-week coincidence.

How we fixed it
  • When hours are unavailable we now annualise payroll and divide evenly (annual ÷ 52), removing the pay-calendar artefact.
  • The compound escalation now requires the condition to hold for two consecutive weeks before being raised to high priority.
Still needs attention
  • The no-hours fallback still depends on payroll granularity; businesses giving only lump-sum runs remain the weakest case.
  • The two-week persistence window is a judgment call to confirm against real labour data.
M-07 · Days Sales Outstanding (DSO) — HIGH›

Discipline: Working capital management / the Cash Conversion Cycle. Source: Gitman (1974); Brealey, Myers & Allen, Ch. 30.

DSO = AR_Balance × 90 / Revenue_90d Cost of DSO = AR_Balance × (annual_cost_of_capital / 365) × extra_days e.g. $87,000 × (0.12/365) × 14 = $5,741/year opportunity cost z_DSO = (DSO_this_week − rolling_12wk_mean) / rolling_12wk_stdev; alert > +2.0 or absolute ceiling (agency 45 days, contractor 70 days)

The 90-day revenue window smooths seasonal swings. The cost-of-DSO converts "getting paid slower" into a dollar opportunity cost that appears nowhere in the financial statements. Confidence HIGH.

Post-revision statusHigh · improved

The averaging fix removes the dominant false-spike cause; in testing, a single fresh invoice that pushed snapshot DSO to ~104 days settled to a true ~50 with averaged AR. The terms-aware ceiling stops nagging businesses operating exactly as contracted. Confident in this metric now.

Discrepancies found: the numerator used a single-day AR snapshot, so issuing one large invoice the day before the weekly run spiked DSO even though collection speed hadn't changed; the absolute ceilings (contractor 70, agency 45) collided with legitimate net-60 contract terms.

How we fixed it
  • Switched the numerator to an average AR over the last four weeks, so one freshly-issued invoice can no longer whipsaw the figure.
  • The ceiling is now a function of the business's own stated payment terms (terms plus a buffer) rather than a flat vertical constant.
Still needs attention
  • The four-week averaging window is a reasonable default but worth confirming for very lumpy B2B billers.
  • Capturing each business's stated payment terms at setup is now a data-collection requirement for the ceiling to work.
M-08 · Per-Client Payment Delay — MEDIUM-HIGH›

Discipline: Individual-level statistical anomaly detection. Source: Chebyshev / normal distribution theory; same method as credit-card fraud detection.

delay_i = paid_date_i − due_date_i (per paid invoice) client_avg_delay = mean(delays); client_stdev = sqrt(sum sq.dev / (n−1)) days_overdue = max(0, today − due_date) client_z = (days_overdue − client_avg_delay) / max(client_stdev, 1) alert when client_z > 2.5 (minimum 3 paid invoices)

Per-client baselines catch the slow creep no aggregate sees. The 2.5 threshold (vs 2.0) compensates for the noisier small-sample standard deviation; the max(stdev,1) floor prevents division by zero for always-on-time clients. Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH, improving past 10 invoices per client.

Post-revision statusMedium-high · improved

The statistical approach is sound and the two changes remove the main false-alarm modes. In testing, a reliable 3-day payer who hit one benign 7-day delay no longer alerts, while a genuine 14-day slip still does. Confidence improves with client history and is now appropriately gated on having enough of it.

Discrepancies found: the max(stdev, 1) floor, while preventing divide-by-zero, manufactured enormous z-scores for very consistent payers the first time they were a little late for a benign reason; the minimum of only three paid invoices made the standard deviation barely meaningful.

How we fixed it
  • Added a practical-significance gate: alert only if z > 2.5 AND the client is at least 10 days past their own norm, so a tiny harmless delay can no longer look like a crisis.
  • Raised the activation minimum from three to six paid invoices; below that the client shows a "limited history" monitor state rather than a confident score.
Still needs attention
  • The 10-day practical floor may want to vary by vertical (a net-15 agency vs a net-60 contractor).
  • Clients with thin history are correctly held back, but coverage is partial until histories build.
M-09 · Vendor Spend Anomaly — HIGH›

Discipline: Statistical process control / Shewhart control charts. Source: Walter Shewhart, Bell Labs, 1924; used in Visa/Mastercard fraud detection.

Per vendor (4+ weeks history): vendor_z = (this_week_charge − rolling_12wk_mean) / rolling_12wk_stdev alert when vendor_z > +2.0 (positive direction only) SaaS case: if stdev < 0.01 × mean, use stdev = 1 (flat-fee vendors)

Each vendor gets its own control chart — a high-variability produce supplier needs a larger deviation to fire; a flat SaaS subscription fires on any change. Confidence HIGH: a 100-year-old, well-validated method, correctly scoped per vendor.

Post-revision statusGood math · not yet ready to alert owners

The per-vendor math is sound and the alert-budget control is the right fix. But this is the metric most exposed to false alarms, so we treat it as ready to MEASURE (shadow mode) but not yet ready to SEND alerts to owners until the budget control is validated on real vendor sets.

Discrepancies found: it ran one independent test per vendor every week, so a business with dozens of vendors had a high chance SOME vendor false-alarmed each week (~60% at 40 vendors) — the single biggest contributor to alert fatigue; usage-based vendors (cloud, usage SaaS) legitimately scale with the business but were tested against a flat mean.

How we fixed it
  • Replaced independent per-vendor testing with a portfolio-level false-discovery control (rank all vendors, surface only the genuinely unusual few) plus a hard cap on vendor alerts per run.
  • Tighten the cutoff as simultaneous tests grow (~2.45σ at 7 vendors, 2.81 at 20, 3.02 at 40) to hold a 5% weekly family-wise rate.
  • Usage-based vendors are now scored on charge-to-revenue ratio rather than raw charge.
Still needs attention
  • The alert-budget parameters (cap size, FDR rate) must be tuned on real businesses — the highest-priority shadow-mode candidate.
  • Tagging usage-based vendors at setup is a new data requirement.
M-10 · Weekly Revenue Trend — MEDIUM›

Discipline: Time-series decomposition / seasonal adjustment. Source: Makridakis, Wheelwright & Hyndman; US Census X-12-ARIMA; NRA Operations Report.

annual_weekly_avg = sum(weekly_revenue over 24mo) / 104 seasonal_index[w] = mean(revenue in week-w observations) / annual_weekly_avg seasonal_expected = annual_weekly_avg × seasonal_index[current_week] revenue_z = (this_week − seasonal_expected) / stdev_for_week[w] alert when revenue_z < −2.0

Prevents the predictable February slump from firing while still catching a genuine non-seasonal decline. Confidence MEDIUM: the most statistically complex calculation — with only ~2 observations per week-of-year from 24 months, indices are imprecise, improving with more years. Disabled under 12 months of history.

Post-revision statusImproved but still weakest — keep working

The pooling fix is a large improvement: in testing, per-week estimates from two points ranged from 10 to 1317 against a true value of 500, while the pooled estimate landed at 516 — usable rather than random. But it remains the most data-hungry metric and the one whose accuracy most improves with more history. Honestly MEDIUM, and flagged as the foundation to keep perfecting.

Discrepancies found: the per-week residual standard deviation was estimated from only ~2 observations per week-of-year — you cannot estimate a standard deviation from two points, so the denominator was effectively random; this is the weakest math in the system AND it feeds the survival metrics (M-01, M-11, M-12), so its noise propagated into metrics labelled high confidence.

How we fixed it
  • Stopped estimating a separate standard deviation per week; we pool the residual variance across all weeks into one stable denominator.
  • Smooth the 52-week seasonal curve (monthly index interpolated, or a low-order seasonal fit) so two noisy yearly points don't define 52 free parameters.
  • Until 3+ years of history exist, we widen the band and explicitly label any revenue-derived cash forecast as lower-confidence.
Still needs attention
  • It genuinely needs more historical data — accuracy rises materially as businesses accumulate a third and fourth year.
  • Because M-01, M-11 and M-12 sit on top of it, it is the single highest-leverage metric to keep improving; the others are gated on it.
M-11 · Hire Affordability Model — HIGH›

Discipline: Scenario-based financial modelling / Canadian employment cost. Source: FP&A what-if analysis; CRA T4032 (2026); Service Canada EI rates.

annual_all_in = annual_salary × (1 + 0.0761) (CPP 5.95% + EI 1.66%) daily_hire_cost = annual_all_in / 365 modified_forecast[d] = baseline_forecast[d] − daily_hire_cost min_balance > $10,000 SAFE | $3k–10k MARGINAL | $0–3k RISKY | ≤0 UNAFFORDABLE Then: find earliest start_date where min_balance > $10,000

Fully loads the role with explicit Canadian employer taxes, re-runs the 90-day model, and finds the earliest safe start date if not immediately affordable. Confidence HIGH: rates are published and exact; output uncertainty equals the M-01 baseline's.

Post-revision statusDependent — park until M-01/M-10 are solid

There is no independent math to be confident in here; it is a wrapper around M-01 plus owner inputs. The revisions make it more realistic, but its accuracy is entirely inherited from the forecast. Recommended to set aside operationally until M-01 and M-10 are solid, at which point it becomes accurate for free.

Discrepancies found: it modelled the hire as a clean daily salary cost only — no onboarding ramp, no one-time setup cost, and no revenue the hire is meant to generate (so every hire looked like pure cost); it rests entirely on the M-01 forecast, so a "Safe" verdict was only as good as a seasonal revenue estimate three months out.

How we fixed it
  • Added the real cash shape of a hire: one-time setup cost, a productivity ramp, and an optional revenue contribution starting in month 2–3.
  • The verdict is now reported against M-01's simulation band ("safe in ~80% of scenarios; tight if Q4 underperforms") rather than a single binary label.
Still needs attention
  • The ramp and revenue-contribution assumptions are owner-supplied and need sensible defaults.
  • It should not be a priority build right now; perfecting M-01 perfects it.
M-12 · Project / Contract Float — MEDIUM-HIGH›

Discipline: Project finance / working-capital float analysis. Source: Peterson, Construction Accounting and Financial Management.

project_forecast[d] = baseline[d] − upfront_costs[d] + payment_inflows[d] expected_payment_date = contractual_date + client_avg_delay (from M-08) min_float_balance = min(project_forecast[d] for d in float_period) > $15,000 safe | < $15,000 recommend credit draw | < 0 cannot self-fund recommended_draw = max(0, 15,000 − min_float_balance)

Tracks the running float between project spend and client payment, applying the per-client delay model to the inflows. Confidence MEDIUM-HIGH: exact given the cost schedule; uncertainty comes from owner-entered milestone accuracy.

Post-revision statusDependent — park until M-01 is solid

Like the hire model, it is a wrapper around M-01 plus owner-entered schedules. The revisions fix the dangerous shortcuts (ignored fixed costs, optimistic payment), and the error now points the safe way. But it has no independent math to be separately confident in, so it is set aside until M-01 is solid.

Discrepancies found: for a first project with a new client it assumed on-time payment — optimistic exactly where the stakes are highest; when the float was collapsed to a simple upfront-cost-minus-balance subtraction, it ignored ordinary payroll and rent accruing during the float, so the real low point was deeper and the recommended draw too small.

How we fixed it
  • We always run the full daily loop across the float window now, including the baseline fixed costs that accrue during it.
  • For unknown clients we apply the vertical's average lateness instead of assuming on-time payment, and size the recommended draw to a "client pays 30 days late" stress case.
Still needs attention
  • The unknown-client lateness prior needs vertical-specific defaults.
  • Multi-milestone / progress-billing projects are a known harder case to detail later.
M-13 · Runway (Founder Mode) — HIGH / MED›

Discipline: Startup financial modelling / burn rate analysis. Source: YC Startup School; a16z; Feld & Mendelson, Venture Deals.

gross_burn = payroll + rent + software + contractors + other opex net_burn = gross_burn − revenue_monthly (lumpy: trailing 3-mo average) runway_months = current_cash / net_burn raise_deadline = today + (runway_months − 6) months alert: runway < 6 "start your raise now" · < 3 "critical"

Negative net burn (profitable) yields an "infinite runway" status. The 6-month buffer reflects that fundraising takes 3–6 months. Confidence HIGH for burn (actual integrated data), MEDIUM for revenue estimate where streams are mixed.

Post-revision statusHigh — once the fix is in

With the normalization fix, this is one of the most testable and demonstrable metrics in the system: a live number compared against a real bank balance over time. Without the fix it was confidently wrong by ~1.8×. The clearest "fix-then-fully-confident" case — the fix is small and the metric becomes a flagship.

Discrepancies found: the single most consequential arithmetic risk in the whole model — if a payroll feed arrives on a non-monthly cycle (biweekly, weekly) and is plugged into a monthly burn total without conversion, burn is understated and runway overstated; a biweekly figure read as monthly overstates runway by roughly 1.8×. This is the headline number founders quote to investors, so the error was maximally visible.

How we fixed it
  • Normalise EVERY payroll feed to a monthly figure at ingestion with an explicit, tested conversion (biweekly ×26/12, weekly ×52/12, semimonthly ×2), asserting the unit so a non-monthly value can never reach a monthly total unconverted.
  • Added a cross-check: if M-13's runway and M-01's cash-gap date disagree (8 months vs a breach in 10 weeks), the contradiction is flagged.
Still needs attention
  • The fix is a hard precondition for testing — validating an unconverted runway would mean validating a number wrong by ~1.8× without knowing it.
  • Net burn for mixed-revenue businesses (consulting + product) still relies on revenue categorisation quality.
M-14 · Break-Even Revenue — HIGH›

Discipline: Cost-volume-profit (CVP) analysis. Source: Harris (1936); Horngren, Datar & Rajan, Ch. 3.

At break-even: 0 = Revenue − Fixed_Costs − (variable_cost_ratio × Revenue) Revenue = Fixed_Costs / (1 − variable_cost_ratio) (1 − variable_cost_ratio) = contribution_margin_ratio e.g. fixed $14,400/wk, variable ratio 0.34: break_even = 14,400 / 0.66 = $21,818/week

Fixed costs identified by recurrence detection; variable ratio from trailing 3-month COGS/revenue. Confidence HIGH: the CVP formula is exact and universal; uncertainty is only in separating fixed from variable costs.

Post-revision statusHigh for the formula · good for the inputs

The CVP formula is exact and universally accepted. The remaining uncertainty is entirely in the cost-classification step, which the range now communicates honestly rather than hiding behind false precision. Confident in the math; the range is the honest expression of the input uncertainty.

Discrepancies found: the weak point was the fixed-vs-variable split — hourly labour is variable but, when paid through the same payroll debit as salaried staff, was tagged as fixed, distorting the ratio and often understating break-even (the dangerous direction: owner thinks they're safe when not); step-fixed costs (fixed until you add a shift or location) were treated as smoothly linear, misleading exactly at expansion-decision volumes.

How we fixed it
  • Split payroll into fixed (salaried) and variable (hourly) using hours/rate data where available, estimating the variable share from the vertical otherwise.
  • Report break-even as a range reflecting the classification uncertainty, and warn when the business is near a known step-cost boundary.
Still needs attention
  • The variable-labour estimate when hours are missing needs vertical defaults.
  • Step-cost boundary detection is a nice-to-have that needs real cost-structure data to do well.
M-15 · Expansion Readiness Score — MEDIUM›

Discipline: Composite index construction / multi-criteria decision analysis. Source: Altman Z-Score (1968); OECD Handbook on Constructing Composite Indicators (2008); HDI, FICO.

Six normalised sub-scores (0–100): dso, margin, coverage, runway, gst, data_quality expansion_score = dso×0.20 + margin×0.20 + coverage×0.20 + runway×0.20 + gst×0.10 + data_quality×0.10 Bands: 80–100 ready | 60–79 nearly | 40–59 needs work | <40 stabilise first For each component < 70 → generate a specific fix

A weighted linear composite — the simplest, most interpretable composite-index method — with DSO, margin, coverage, and runway weighted equally as the primary credit/investment screening criteria. Confidence MEDIUM: weights are a designed prior, not statistically fitted; they can later be tuned via logistic regression against loan-approval outcomes.

Post-revision statusLowest — finish last, by design

Honestly MEDIUM and, because it sits on top of every other metric, it can be no more reliable than its inputs. The revisions make it honest (band not decimal, confidence-weighted, labelled indicative). It is the last thing to finish, not the first — it cannot be properly validated until there is real outcome data to fit against.

Discrepancies found: the weights were asserted from a view of what banks emphasise, not fitted to any outcome, yet the score was reported to two decimals — false precision on what is essentially an informed opinion; it aggregates the other metrics, so every upstream error (the runway unit bug, the seasonal noise) was baked in, and confidence was not propagated.

How we fixed it
  • Stopped reporting a false-precise decimal; now show a band plus the component bars.
  • Down-weight components whose inputs are low-confidence (runway from a messy revenue base, seasonal-dependent metrics).
  • Labelled it "indicative, not predictive" until real outcome data (actual loan approvals/declines) lets us fit the weights with a regression.
Still needs attention
  • Blocked on real outcome data for weight-fitting; until then it stays indicative.
  • It inherits every upstream metric's accuracy, so its improvement is a downstream consequence of perfecting the others, not a separate workstream.

Cross-Cutting Fixes

Two fixes that span every z-score metric

Fix 01 · alert-budget control

False-discovery, not independent tests

Instead of testing every metric and vendor independently at a fixed cutoff — which makes some alarm trip almost every week — we cap and rank alerts so only the genuinely unusual few reach the owner, with a stricter cutoff as more things are watched at once. This is the main defence against alert fatigue and the reason several metrics are "ready to measure" before they are "ready to send."

Fix 02 · extremes are data-quality flags

Verify the data before declaring a crisis

Extreme readings are now treated as data-quality flags first, financial signals second. Beyond about four standard deviations, a reading is more likely a categorisation error or double-count than a true event, so we route it to "verify this data" rather than asserting a cost crisis or collapse.

Overall Posture

The formulas were strong; the revisions made the numbers honest

Across almost every metric, the arithmetic was already correct. What changed was rarely the core formula and almost always one of three things — how uncertainty is reported (point estimate to band), how thresholds behave on small or skewed samples (sigma floors, t-cutoffs, practical-significance gates), or a unit/eligibility error pushed back toward the safe direction (runway normalization, GST eligibility).

The dependency structure is the thing to hold in mind M-10 (seasonal) feeds M-01, which feeds M-02, M-11 and M-12; and M-15 aggregates almost everything. So effort spent perfecting M-11, M-12 or M-15 right now is largely wasted — they improve automatically when their foundations do. The highest-leverage work is M-10 and M-01.

The residual errors that remain are, by design, pointed in the safe direction: GST over-reserves rather than under-reserves, payroll coverage tests the worst-realistic balance, project float assumes lateness, break-even communicates a range. A tool that errs toward caution is the correct posture for small-business survival decisions.

Section 4 · Property-Based Testing

The four kinds of rule under test

Guards

If an upstream input is None or structurally dirty (a missing day, a malformed list), the metric must return a guarded result — never crash, never compute on bad data. Tested on M-15 and M-01 across every subset of missing inputs.

Bounds

Outputs must stay inside their mathematically valid range: runway ≥ 0, gross margin ≤ 100, payroll coverage ≥ 0. A violation means the arithmetic produced something impossible.

Determinism

The Monte Carlo cash-gap model must return bit-exact identical output for identical inputs and seed. Verified across 200 generated input sets — 400,000 simulation runs, zero drift. This is the foundation of the whole "deterministic math" promise.

Metamorphic

Relations that must hold without knowing the exact answer: gross margin is scale-free (doubling revenue and COGS leaves it unchanged); more cash always means longer runway; bigger payroll always means lower coverage. These test the reasoning, not just the plumbing.

A representative property

Metamorphic scaling invariance for gross margin — a pure mathematical truth that a sign error or operator swap would violate:

@given( revenue = st.floats(min_value=1, max_value=1_000_000), cogs = st.floats(min_value=0, max_value=1_000_000), ) @settings(max_examples=300) def test_m04_scaling_invariance(revenue, cogs): """Gross margin is scale-free: doubling revenue AND cogs leaves it unchanged.""" base = m04_gross_margin(revenue, cogs) scaled = m04_gross_margin(revenue * 2, cogs * 2) if base is None or scaled is None: return # a guard fired - acceptable assert math.isclose(base, scaled)

Hypothesis generated 300 input pairs; the relation held on every one. If it hadn't, it would print the smallest pair that breaks it. Full suite: test_properties.py ↗

Per-Partner Rationale

Why these integrations, specifically

The integration stack encodes a thesis about the Canadian SMB market. Three choices in particular are deliberate and worth stating plainly.

ADP over Gusto for Canada

The payroll decision

Gusto holds roughly 1.25% Canadian market share — it is a US-tech-startup tool. ADP Run is the default for Canadian SMBs with 5–50 employees, used by over a million Canadian businesses. Building Canada-first means ADP-first. Treating Gusto as the default is a common and costly misconception.

Square over Toast for Canada

The POS decision

Toast dominates US full-service restaurants but has thin Canadian coverage. Square has a much stronger Canadian footprint across quick-service, cafes, food trucks, and on-site contractors. For most Canadian operators, Square is the realistic POS connection — Toast is supported but secondary.

Stripe never for restaurants

The revenue-source decision

Restaurants take payment through Square or Toast, not Stripe. Stripe is the correct revenue source only for agencies (Sarah) and e-commerce (David). Mapping the right revenue integration to the right vertical is what makes the forecast specific rather than generic.

After 12 months, the owner's entire financial history — categorised transactions, seasonal patterns, per-client payment behaviour — lives in Pulse. Switching means losing all of it.
